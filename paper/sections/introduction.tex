\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The global population aged 60 years and older is projected to double by 2050, exceeding 2 billion individuals. Simultaneously, the World Health Organization anticipates a shortage of 10 million skilled health professionals by 2030. Physical rehabilitation is particularly affected; in numerous low- and middle-income countries, the physiotherapist-to-population ratio is less than 1 per 100,000. Consequently, millions of elderly patients lack access to supervised exercise therapy for conditions including stroke recovery, osteoarthritis, and fall prevention.

Computer vision-based rehabilitation systems have shown potential to improve physiotherapy services~\cite{chen2020pose}. However, existing systems face several challenges in serving elderly users: (1) dependence on expensive depth sensors such as Kinect or LiDAR, which are typically unavailable in home settings; (2) exclusive reliance on body-only 2D pose estimation, neglecting hand gestures and facial expressions that are critical for assessing exercise form; (3) absence of natural-language voice interfaces suitable for individuals with limited digital literacy; (4) inadequate adaptation to elderly-specific requirements, such as reduced mobility, heightened pain sensitivity, and slower exercise pace; and (5) generic feedback mechanisms that fail to consider individual clinical conditions.

This paper introduces \system{}, a multimodal AI rehabilitation system designed to address these barriers. The scope is defined explicitly at the outset: the paper presents a \emph{systems feasibility} study that shows the integration to be computationally practical and architecturally robust, rather than offering clinical validation. The contributions are threefold. First, a kinematics and scoring algorithm pipeline integrates real-time whole-body 3D pose estimation via RTMW3D-L~\cite{jiang2024rtmw} from MMPose~\cite{contributors2020openmmlab} with quaternion-based joint angle computation, SPARC smoothness~\cite{Scholp2021}, constrained DTW alignment, and temporal compensation detection, organized within a clinically-inspired six-dimension scoring framework. Second, a multi-indicator facial state detection pipeline leverages RTMW3D's 68 face landmarks and OpenFace~3.0 Action Unit detection, combining PSPI-based pain estimation~\cite{prkachin2008structure}, PERCLOS-based fatigue monitoring, blink and yawn detection, and engagement analysis~\cite{6786307}, with automatic fallback to geometric analysis. Third, the proposed scoring framework is evaluated on two public rehabilitation benchmarks, yielding a moderate but statistically significant correlation with clinical scores on KIMORE (Spearman $\rho = 0.347$, $p < 10^{-11}$) and strong exercise discriminability on UCO (AUC = 0.974), with position DTW outperforming angle DTW by an order of magnitude.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} details the system architecture and algorithms. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} reports scoring-framework validation on public benchmarks. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the design rationale and the limitations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with future directions.
