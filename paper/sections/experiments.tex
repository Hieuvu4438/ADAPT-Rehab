\section{Experimental Setup and Evaluation}
\label{sec:experiments}

\system{} is positioned as a \emph{systems feasibility} study: the goal of this evaluation is to establish that real-time, whole-body, multimodal rehabilitation analysis is achievable on a single consumer GPU, and to characterize honestly which components are mature and which require further validation. Two sets of results are therefore reported, namely (1)~the temporal stability of the pose pipeline and (2)~the scoring validation on two public rehabilitation benchmarks. No claim of clinical-grade joint-angle accuracy is made: as detailed below, the absence of metric-scale ground truth on the exercise data means that the pose metrics measure self-consistency rather than error against motion-capture references.

\subsection{Datasets}
The evaluation draws on three public rehabilitation datasets, each serving a distinct purpose. KIMORE~\cite{Capecci2019} comprises 378 recordings of 5 rehabilitation exercises performed by 78 subjects (44 healthy and 34 with motor dysfunctions), captured with Kinect~v2 (25-joint skeleton); each recording carries a clinical Total Score (cTS) assigned by physical therapists, and the dataset is used here for clinical correlation and component ablation. UCO Physical Rehabilitation~\cite{AguilarOrtega2023} contains 393 recordings of 16 exercises from 27 subjects, captured with 5 synchronized RGB cameras and an OptiTrack motion-capture ground-truth system ($\pm$0.5\,mm accuracy), accompanied by 1{,}918 per-repetition clinician scores on a 2--5 scale, and is used for exercise discrimination and cross-view analysis. UI-PRMD~\cite{Vakanski2018} provides 10 rehabilitation exercises performed by 10 healthy subjects, captured synchronously with a Kinect sensor and a Vicon optical motion-capture system; it is used to assess the temporal stability of the pose pipeline.

\subsection{Pose Estimation Temporal Stability}
The reported metric is \emph{temporal self-consistency}: the mean per-joint deviation of each frame's 3D keypoints from the per-video temporal mean, which quantifies frame-to-frame jitter and tracking stability. On UI-PRMD, this self-consistency measure over 1{,}423 Kinect-skeleton samples yields $0.18 \pm 0.22$\,mm deviation, confirming that the mapping framework runs end-to-end across skeleton formats with sub-millimetre jitter.

\subsection{Scoring Validation on KIMORE}
The scoring framework is evaluated on KIMORE using a fixed-reference protocol: the top-10 clinically-scored recordings per exercise serve as expert templates (mirroring clinical practice, where a physiotherapist demonstration serves as the reference), and Procrustes-aligned position DTW computes the similarity of each recording to the nearest expert template.

Table~\ref{tab:kimore_corr} reports the Spearman correlation between the proposed score and the clinical Total Score. All five exercises show a statistically significant positive correlation ($p < 0.05$), with an overall $\rho = 0.347$ ($p < 10^{-11}$).

\subsection{Exercise Discrimination on UCO}
Exercise-level discriminability is evaluated on UCO using 6{,}000 balanced same-exercise vs.\ different-exercise pairs across 16 exercise classes. Table~\ref{tab:uco_discrim} reports a same-vs-different AUC of $0.974$ with a 48-point mean separation, indicating that the scoring framework reliably distinguishes correct exercise performance from incorrect cross-exercise comparisons.

\begin{table}[htbp]

Cross-view analysis. Silhouette-based motion signals exhibit an 87-point similarity drop across the five camera views, reinforcing the motivation for direct 3D pose estimation in rehabilitation settings where camera placement varies.
