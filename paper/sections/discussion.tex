\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Whole-Body Pose Estimation Benefits}
RTMW3D-L's 133-keypoint output, comprising 17 body, 6 foot, 68 face, and 42 hand keypoints, enables simultaneous monitoring of hand gestures for exercise-form verification and facial expressions for pain detection. This comprehensive whole-body coverage is particularly valuable for rehabilitation exercises involving hand placement, such as weight-bearing poses, and for detecting compensatory movements indicated by facial grimacing.

RTMW3D-L's published Human3.6M accuracy of 40.9~mm MPJPE (reported by the model's authors under ground-truth 2D input~\cite{jiang2024rtmw}) is competitive with transformer methods such as MotionBERT (35.2~mm) while running at higher throughput, making whole-body feedback responsive enough for interactive use.

\subsection{Multi-Indicator Facial State Detection}
The facial state detection pipeline integrates multiple indicators beyond simple AU detection: PSPI-based pain estimation, PERCLOS-based fatigue monitoring, blink and yawn detection, and engagement analysis. Together, these provide a more comprehensive assessment of the user's physiological and psychological state during exercise.

The priority-based classification system (Pain $>$ Exhaustion $>$ Fatigue $>$ Boredom $>$ Normal) ensures that critical states receive attention first. Temporal smoothing (mode over the last 90 frames) reduces false positives from transient facial expressions.

\subsection{Clinical Relevance}
Quaternion-based joint angles address the multi-plane movement limitations of dot-product methods, which is critical for shoulder and hip rehabilitation where movements span multiple anatomical planes. ISB-convention clinical angles (0 ° = full extension) align with established clinical measurement practices.

The six-dimension scoring framework provides clinicians with an interpretable, multi-faceted breakdown rather than a single opaque number. All six dimensions and their weights are clinically inspired but have not yet been calibrated against physiotherapist assessments or patient outcomes. The framework serves as a structured, transparent scaffold, with its weighting and clinical validation remaining open problems.

\subsection{Scoring Validation}
The DTW-based scoring framework achieves a statistically significant positive correlation with clinical scores on KIMORE (Spearman $\rho = 0.347$, $p < 10^{-11}$) across all five rehabilitation exercises, with Procrustes-aligned position DTW ($\rho = 0.400$) outperforming angle-based DTW ($\rho = 0.042$) by an order of magnitude. This indicates that body-level spatial alignment captures rehabilitation movement quality better than per-joint angle trajectories---a finding with practical implications for system design.

On UCO, the same framework achieves strong exercise discriminability (AUC = 0.974, 48-point separation between same-exercise and different-exercise pairs), indicating that DTW-based scoring reliably distinguishes correct from incorrect exercise performance. However, clinical correlation on UCO is weaker ($\rho \approx 0.08$--$0.18$), partly because UCO recordings contain only three tracked joints per exercise (the exercise-relevant kinematic chain), which limits the information available to position-based DTW. Kinematic smoothness (jerk, $\rho = 0.166$) proves a stronger predictor than DTW similarity on UCO, consistent with the clinical emphasis on movement quality over template matching for single-joint exercises.

The cross-dataset pattern---position DTW $>$ angle DTW on whole-body KIMORE, but angle DTW $>$ position DTW on sparse-joint UCO---suggests that the optimal similarity metric depends on the available kinematic information. Systems targeting whole-body exercises should prioritize spatial alignment, while those restricted to single kinematic chains may benefit more from angle-based comparison.

\subsection{Elderly-Specific Design Choices}
Several design decisions specifically address elderly user needs: (1)~Wait-for-User synchronization allows unlimited time at each exercise checkpoint, preventing the system from rushing users; (2)~Safe-Max calibration establishes individual ROM limits that constrain exercises to the user's physical capability; (3)~voice guidance with natural-language, respectful address forms accommodates literacy barriers and cultural expectations across deployment contexts; (4)~automatic exercise pause on moderate or severe pain detection provides an unsupervised safety mechanism essential for home-based rehabilitation.
